The insight was correct: small businesses are chronically underserved by traditional legal services. Hourly billing creates perverse incentives that punish clients for asking questions. Most small business legal work is repetitive and plannable.

We built a beautiful product. Our attorneys were excellent. Our NPS was 71.

The California Bar Association had questions about our fee structure. Specifically, about whether our business model constituted unauthorized practice of law in the fee-splitting sense. We had consulted our own attorneys on this, which in retrospect was not the most independent analysis.

We modified our structure. We modified it again. We filed responses. We engaged bar counsel.

In parallel, our per-attorney client capacity — the core unit economics of the model — proved harder to improve than our financial model projected. Attorneys who can handle 50 monthly clients well struggle to handle 100. The relationship quality that made Atlas valuable was incompatible with the scale Atlas required.

We shut down in 2020 after four years. Our clients received 60-day notices and referrals to traditional firms. Several of those attorneys still refer to their time at Atlas as the best work environment they ever had.

This was not sufficient to save the company. It was not nothing, either.
