The research was on our side. Studies show handwritten letters are perceived as more meaningful than digital messages. People report wanting more deliberate, slow communication in their lives. Every focus group we ran produced enthusiastic responses.

The conversion data told a different story.

Our average subscriber opened the stationery box with genuine delight. They placed the paper on their desk. They thought warmly about the people they intended to write to. They opened their phone. They sent a voice memo.

We tried everything. We added prompts. We added a mobile app that made writing feel more like texting. We added "letter starter" templates. We added guilt, in the form of a weekly email that said things like "Your paper is waiting." This last intervention did not help retention and generated our worst NPS scores.

In 2020, we launched a pandemic pivot toward "therapeutic correspondence" — letters to strangers matched by mood and interest. This worked beautifully and had nothing to do with our core business model.

We shut down in 2021. Our Fabriano paper supplier sent a handwritten note. It was, genuinely, the best piece of correspondence we ever received.
