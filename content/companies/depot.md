The last-hundred-yards problem is real. Package theft, missed deliveries, inaccessible addresses, the particular anxiety of waiting for something that might or might not arrive — these are genuine frictions in urban life.

We solved them, at small scale, in a way that genuinely worked.

Our pilot in Portland's Buckman neighborhood had 340 registered users, 12 nodes, and a Net Promoter Score of 63. Neighbors used it to receive packages and also to send cookies to each other across the block, which was not in our pitch deck but was the thing we were proudest of.

The hardware was the problem. Each node cost $2,400 to manufacture, $800 to install, $120/month to maintain, and required a reliable local partner willing to explain it to their customers at no additional compensation. Our partner churn was 40% annually.

We raised a seed round. We raised a bridge round. We explored a partnership with a national coffee chain. We explored a partnership with a national apartment operator. We explored a partnership with USPS, which involved a nine-month contracting process, a pilot approval committee, and then a change in regional leadership that reset the relationship.

In 2022, Amazon expanded its Hub Counter program. The problem we had identified remained real. The path to outlasting the competition did not.

The Portland nodes ran until March 2023. The corner store on Ankeny Street still has one. It displays "OFFLINE" but nobody has moved it.
