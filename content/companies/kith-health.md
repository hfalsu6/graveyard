Kith was built for a specific person: competent, busy, avoidant. Someone who has let a concerning symptom go six months because scheduling a primary care appointment requires a phone call made during business hours to a practice that is booked until November.

That person exists in large numbers. They became our members. They loved us deeply and referred their friends and wrote reviews that made our patient experience team cry, in the good way.

The problem was the math.

Healthcare economics are not designed for what we were doing. Our per-member cost of care was manageable at scale we did not yet have. Our clinical staff — excellent, committed, idealistic — could handle a panel size that produced sustainable revenue only if member acquisition costs were near zero, which they were not.

We raised $18M. We served 12,000 members. We extended our runway four times. We had the best NPS score in our competitive set and the worst unit economics.

In 2024, after five years, our lead investors declined to fund a Series B. We explored acquirers. We found several interested parties. None of them wanted to preserve the care model. We declined.

The clinicians landed well. The members received 90-day transition notices. The in-home visit was, to the end, the thing people mentioned most.
